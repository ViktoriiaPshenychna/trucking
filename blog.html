HOW TO PREPARE FOR THE NEW ENTRANT AUDIT?

Within the first 12 months of getting your operating authority, you WILL be audited by the FMCSA as part of their New Entrant Safety Assurance Program.

The following 14 violations will result in an automatic failure of the audit, so pay close attention to these areas:

  1. Failing to implement an alcohol and/or controlled substances testing program.

  2. Driving or using a driver with an alcohol content of 0.04 or greater.

  3. Using a driver known to have tested positive for a controlled substance.

  4. Driving or using a driver with a suspended, revoked, or canceled license or a federal disqualification.

  5. Not carrying appropriate insurance, including minimum cargo ($5,000 per vehicle and $10,000 per occurrence) and $750,000 primary liability.

  6. Failing to require a driver to keep record of duty status current.

  7. Failing to correct out-of-service defects listed on vehicle inspection reports before further operation.

  8. Failing to implement a random controlled substance testing program.

  9. Using a driver who has refused to submit to an alcohol or controlled substances test.

  10. Driving or using a driver without a CDL.

  11. Allowing a disqualified driver to drive.

  12. Knowingly using a physically unqualified driver.

  13. Running a vehicle declared out-of-service before repairs are made.

  14. Running a truck not inspected periodically.

WHATâ€™S THE PROCESS OF GETTING AN MC NUMBER?

Are you ready to kickstart your trucking business and secure an MC number for your operations? Navigating the complex process of obtaining an MC number can be overwhelming, but fear not! We've got you covered with a streamlined approach to guide you every step of the way.

  • Step #1 - USDOT Registration: Start by registering with the U.S. Department of Transportation (USDOT). Provide essential details about your vehicles, cargo, and business to receive a USDOT number.

  • Step #2 - Apply for MC Number: Once you have your USDOT number, apply for your MC number through the FMCSA. This involves careful attention to paperwork and selecting the right type of operating authority for your business.

  • Step #3 - BOC-3 Filing: Ensure your BOC-3 form, designating a process agent for legal paperwork, is filed by a reliable agent or bookkeeping company.

  • Step #4 - Unified Carrier Registration (UCR): Pay the UCR fee based on the size of your fleet if you operate across state lines.

  • Step #5 - Obtain Insurance and Intrastate Permits: Secure proper insurance tailored to your trucking business and obtain necessary intrastate permits.

  • Step #6 - Expert Permitting Assistance: Our team of experts can simplify the permitting process, helping you with over 20 years of trucking industry experience.

FOUR QUICK TIPS FOR SELECTING AN INSURANCE COMPANY

Being your own boss in the trucking industry is rewarding, but choosing the right insurance company is crucial for protecting your business. Here are four essential tips to help you make the right decision:

  1. Inquire about the insurer's response time to get your truck back on the road swiftly. As your income depends on driving and hauling, any delays in getting your truck repaired could lead to increased costs. Additionally, check if your policy includes coverage for a truck rental while your rig is in the shop.

  2. Investigate the insurance company's credentials via AM Best, where you can access their ratings after setting up a profile, a free process. AM Best evaluates insurers based on their financial stability. It is recommended to focus on A-rated insurers, as they typically provide superior coverage and expedite the processing of claims.

  3. Assess the insurance agent's accessibility during emergencies. Some carriers use a 1-800 number for claims, leading to longer response times and less personalized service. Check if the insurer offers a 24-hour helpline with live representatives. Ensure you have access to your agent's cell phone number for quicker assistance. Request information about the post-accident process to determine if you can speak directly with a person or need to leave a voicemail, especially after regular business hours, like 5:00 PM.

  4. Stay in regular communication with your insurance agent after selecting a provider. Inform them promptly about any changes, such as new equipment, routes, drivers, or location. Failure to update your agent about changes to rigs, mileage, etc., may lead to insufficient coverage or policy cancellation in case of a claim. If you plan to transport something unusual, like a reefer load while primarily operating as a dry van hauler, be sure to inform your agent about this as well. Keeping your agent informed will help ensure your insurance policy accurately reflects your business's needs and reduces the risk of coverage gaps or issues in the future.

If you require assistance in finding insurance, trusted companies like ours have extensive networks of preferred agencies. These agencies can provide you with the appropriate coverage tailored to your business needs.

WHAT IS AN MCS-150 FORM?

The USDOT number is a unique identifier that allows for collecting and monitoring a companyâ€™s safety information. This information is provided during audits, inspections, compliance reviews, and crash investigations.

The information associated with the USDOT number must be updated every two years. The update can be done using an updated Motor Carrier Identification Report called an MCS-150 form. This form allows the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration to gather updated information and create a score based on compliance, safety, and accountability.

The MCS-150 biennial update ensures that information about your company is updated, including the miles driven, any additions to your fleet, and related safety information. The FMCSA requires that trucking company owners submit an update every two years, but when the update is due may vary.

You can determine when to submit your updated MCS-150 by looking at your USDOT number. The last two digits of the number tell you when you must file. The last digit represents your filing month, and the number before the last is your year of filing. The last digit and corresponding months are: 1 - January, 2 - February, 3 - March, 4 - April, 5 - May, 6 - June, 7 - July, 8 - August, 9 - September, 0 - October.

Who is Required to Provide an MCS-150 Biennial Update?

Companies operating commercial vehicles for interstate operations by transporting passengers or hauling cargo must be registered with the FMCSA and have a USDOT number. You must have this number and update your information using the MCS-150 form if your vehicle is used to transport the types and quantities of hazardous materials requiring a safety permit in intrastate commerce.

In addition to this provision, companies must file an updated MCS-150 if they meet the following conditions and are involved in interstate commerce:

  1. Have a gross vehicle rating or gross combination weight of 10,001 pounds or more.
  2. Have a vehicle designed to transport eight or more individuals.
